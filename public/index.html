Distracted Driving

Andrew Leffingwell, Jayden D'Souza, Noah Zhao

Distracted driving is a serious issue. In 2023 alone, 3,275 people were killed in distracted driving collisions. Additionally, the rate of visible driver phone usage has increased dramatically since 2006, as evident in Chart 1. Although many drivers avoid phone usage for safety reasons, there are many other prominent forms of distracted driving. This is especially noticeable when looking at rear-end collisions, which are frequently caused by distracted drivers in high-traffic situations, as shown by Chart 2.

To better understand the causes of distracted driving, we will introduce a simple traffic simulation. In this simulation, cars travel at a constant speed on a circular road. Without any distractions, every car maintains the optimal speed and there is no traffic buildup.

However, we will now introduce an external distraction. This distraction could be anything from a traffic stop to a beautiful sunset—anything that causes drivers to slow down. In the simulation, this is portrayed by the sun icon, with the yellow circle representing the area of effect for cars. Inside this zone, cars slow down to view the distraction. Try moving the distraction around to see its effects.

In addition to external distractions, many drivers experience distractions from within their own vehicles. To represent these drivers, we will introduce a distracted driver. This car, highlighted in blue, has a delayed reaction to the initial start. This delay could be due to phone usage or another distraction type. Although the initial delay is relatively minor, it compounds as traffic moves forward. Over time, this single delay creates a never-ending traffic loop.

Some people believe they are great multitaskers, but the data indicates otherwise. To test this, we will examine your own distracted driving abilities. Can you maintain the red car’s speed while distracted?

Operate the red car with the accelerate button. After some time, a memory game will appear, forcing you to split your attention between the road and the game. Although the simulation runs in “slow motion” and uses fewer cars to make the task easier, you’ll likely notice that your performance in both activities declines when multitasking.

This final chart compares all scenarios at once, showing how each distraction slows traffic relative to the baseline. If a line doesn’t appear, simply rerun that simulation—the chart uses only the most recent data available.

When you place all the simulations together, a clear story emerges. Whether it’s a scenic view, a delayed reaction, or the added cognitive load of a memory game, each distraction type introduces a slowdown that spreads through the entire system. What feels like a small moment of distraction to one driver becomes a chain reaction for every car behind them.
